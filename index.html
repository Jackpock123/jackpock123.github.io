Earlier I mentioned how construction is slow moving and it likes using standardisation and repeatability to reduce costs. The problem with doing things the way they’ve always been done is that you tend to stagnate and miss opportunities to innovate.

A big missed opportunity in construction is project and commercial management. Simply put, the tools are dated and require a huge amount of manpower and capital to run. Resulting in inaccurate information and making company fees uncompetitive as these costs have to be paid for by the client.

Most importantly when capital and resources are being gobbled up with time consuming administrative tasks, they’re not being allocated to areas which can add value, like improving the actual product their selling.

With regards to construction, these resources could facilitate better value engineering, essentially making a leaner product with less economic and environmental impact. For example: do we need that much concrete? Are we using the right grade of steel? Can the metro station be two levels instead of three?

These are the type of questions we should be asking and I believe that technology can free up more time and resources to do so.

And this is just the one industry I’ve been exposed to. I can imagine there are many more facing the same challenge.

Technical takeaways

The process of building this application has been awesome and I learnt a lot using JavaScript to build collapsible buttons, a tab display, a responsive navigation bar and an image carousel.

I took the decision to enroll in OneMonth’s JavaScript course in August after struggling with freeCodeCamp and it helped provide a much clearer picture of the fundamentals of JavaScript and what it can be used for. Such as what is an API and why would we want to make calls to it.

The JavaScript course's learning objectives contrasted nicely with those of Codewars, where the challenges centered more around using specific technical skills to solve problems with different data types.

The Training JS and Coding Meetup series by myjinxin2015 and PiotrBerebecki were very useful for learning fundamental but yet powerful JS methods such as map(), filter(), reduce() and split() and using E6 features such as arrow functions.

Takeaways from the design process

Designing the user journey was a valuable exercise and I learnt the importance of establishing a hierarchy of information and removing content that distracted from the story I wanted to tell my audience.

I received feedback that my first layout contained too much information and was disorientating because it had lots of links to my personal website. Late in the design process I decided to separate out my application almost completely in an attempt to make the user journey more linear.

Don’t write copy in your text editor! Refreshing web pages to see each tiny update is a sure way to fry your brain and give you writers block!

Make commits more frequently and with useful notes. Originally I was definitely guilty of committing infrequently and with many changes, preferring to use the Jekyll developing platform to review changes locally. When GitHub Desktop stopped working, it gave me a real fright as I had no record of what I’d changed and had to check each file and folder individually.

Jekyll

I realised quickly that if I wanted blogging functionality for my website, having to hardcode every new post would be inefficient and messy when updating the website. I discovered Jekyll, a framework that generates static and blog-aware sites which let me host my website for free on GitHub.

Jekyll is written in Ruby, and allowed me to build my static website from dynamic components such as templates, liquid code and markdown. I made some basic sketches of how I wanted the website to look and using the tutorials from cloudcannon and skills from the OneMonth course I started to build my website.

In August 2012 I started my career as a civil engineer in Atkins Dubai based Ground Engineering team and was fortunate to work on some incredible multi-billion $$$ projects including Doha Metro and Riyadh Metro.

A very happy site engineer checking the seating in the Opera House

I worked mainly in a techincal design role but I was also Assistant Project Manager on Doha Metro's Gold Line where I was responsible along with the Project Manager for a budget of over £1.3m and leading a team of over 40 people spread across three design centres in Dubai, Doha and Manila.

I also spent six months on site as a Site Engineer on the incredible Dubai Opera House and worked in Xi'an for one of China's most prestigious rail design institutes on a three month secondment.

In December 2016 after 4.5 years of professional development and a successful professional review one of my proudest achievement was becoming a chartered member of the Institution of Civil Engineers (CEng MICE).

Becoming bilingual has become a huge passion project of mine. Seeing the joy and infinite opportunity that communcation can unlock all over the world was and continues to be one of my biggest inspirations.

After a 3 month secondment in Xi'an in 2015, I vowed to return to take on Mandarin and arrived for four months in winter 2018, passing my HSK2.

However, during my travels around Latin America in autumn 2017 I fell in love with Spanish and in November 2018 I relocated to Madrid to immerse myself in the language, eventually settling back in Colombia for a few months in July this year.

I've absolutely loved the process of learning through immersion and driving my own study. Instead of traditional classroom based learning I worked with a personal tutor to study situations such as dining out, directing a taxi driver or dating.

This context driven learning approach was so effective for me and in a month I was able to confidently use Spanish in important situations in Madrid (ordering tapas and beer) and my confidence skyrocketed.

I believe confidence is everything when learning another language and that using your daily experiences to dictate what you need to learn is the best way to improve it rapidly.

When leaving Dubai I had a strong desire to explore new places, push myself outside of my comfort zone and hopefully in the process of getting lost have a few experiences that I'd remember for a long time.
